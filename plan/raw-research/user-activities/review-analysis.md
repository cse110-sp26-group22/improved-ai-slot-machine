To understand users better, we can take a look at some slot machine game reviews to see what users like/dislike about slot machine games.

We'll consider both free and paid slot machine games.

### Case 1 - Quick Hit Casino Slots Games, Google Play
Downloads: 10M

App Description: 
> Quick Hit casino slots is the ultimate free Vegas slots experience for mobile, the best classic slot machine games are just a tap away. Enjoy free spinning and winning these free slot machine games!

Factors that users enjoy:
-  Winning rolls are expressed in dollars, emulating a real slot machine
    - This may indicate that we should move away from using virtual currencies like tokens, since they aren't as relatable to the user
- Players enjoy the frequency at which they win
    - If our game isn't tied to real-life monetary rewards, we might want to consider moving the odds in the user's favor to give them more dopamine hits
- The role-play aspect of being a really wealthy person was popular
- Users loved watching the flashy animations and graphics

Factors that users dislike:
- Parts of this app did not have real-world analogues as casino machines, which breaks immersion
- App lag and rendering issues were frequent complaints 
    - This demonstrates the importance of both accessibility and making a performant product

### Case 2 - CloverPit, Steam
Steam Reviews: 11,288 
Note: Steam does not publish the download count of their games

App Description:
> A rogue-lite slot machine nightmare. Gamble for your life in a never-ending debt simulator!

Factors that users enjoy:
- The roguelike nature of the game helped consistently pull players
- The atmosphere and story were consistently loved
    - Many users enjoyed the luck-based nature of the game simply because it helped contribute to the atmosphere
- Players enjoyed being able to simulate gambling without losing real money
- The game loop was consistently rewarding

Factors that users dislike:
- The game leans more on the side of luck than skill
- The set of consistent, viable strategies is pretty small
- Many minor details detract from the UX; some QoL changes would have been preferred
- A lot of upgrades don't feel meaningful or don't offer much in terms of progression

### Analysis:
The users of the free game wanted the app to mimic the real world well.
- In the context of many slot machine apps competing for the top spot in a massive app store, game developers are incentivized to focus on mass appeal.
- This means that fewer risks are taken, adhering closely to actual slot machines.

Alternatively, the users of the paid game were interested in what was unique about the slot machine game.
- These users are paying to have a novel experience, rather than a standard one.
- Such desires come in multiple forms, where you can see one group of players who are more interested in a compelling narrative, whereas other players are more interested in the strategic aspect of the game.

A common trend was that of **fantasy fulfillment**.
- Users wanted to play both games to understand the experience of the in-universe player character.
- The ability to recklessly gamble without losing real money was a strong pull.
- Furthermore, users loved the thrill of massive payouts.

Another common pattern was minor details breaking immersion.
- To make a solid app, the UX needs to be clean and polished.
- Any action which does not feel natural will hurt users' enjoyment.

To be successful, a slot machine game should market itself as selling an *experience* rather than selling just a slot machine interface.
